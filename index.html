Arrests in NYC are down sharply this year. But it's not all due to COVID.

by Aria Velasquez
11.16.20

103,376. That is the number of documented arrests the New York Police Department claims to have made as of September 30 of this year. Why does this number stand out? Because it’s less than half the number of arrests made by NYPD last year and roughly one-quarter the number of arrests made by NYPD a decade ago. If the police department continues on its current trajectory, NYPD is on track to end 2020 with less than 200,000 recorded arrests. According to data from NYC’s Open Data Portal, arrests in New York City declined an average of 7.11% between 2011 and 2019. This year, year-over-year arrests are down almost 52%. (NYPD did not respond to multiple requests for comment by press time for this story. This story will be updated in the event that they do respond.)

How much of the drop in arrests is due to the coronavirus pandemic versus other factors is hard to say. April 2020, the only full month where New Yorkers were on lockdown, saw just under 8,300 arrests, but it wasn’t the lowest number of the year. In June of this year, after the city started reopening, NYPD made 8,083 arrests, and fewer still (7,461) in July. What is known is that arrests in NYC have been dropping steadily over the last decade, and this year is on track to have the lowest number of arrests since 2006 (the earliest year for which open data is available without a FOIA request).

“We did see there was a correlation between the decrease of cases coming into arraignment court corresponded with the post-Memorial Day/George Floyd-related decrease in arrests,” says Jullian Harris-Calvin, a program director at the Vera Institute. The Vera Institute is a nonprofit organization that focuses on “securing equal justice, ending mass incarceration, and strengthening families and communities.” Harris-Calvin’s observation squares with the available data. All other years for which data is available show that arrests usually decrease slightly in June and increase to a lesser degree in July. This year, arrests were down 40.48% month-over-month in June and 7.70% month-over-month in July.

This year's drop has not gone unnoticed by casual observers, academics, and city power brokers alike. While it is illegal for police and other public employees in New York State to engage in work slowdowns or stoppages, the available data shows that a slowdown happened anyway this summer around the time activists took to the streets to call for defunding the police. Such tactics by the NYPD are not unheard of. There was a less extreme drop in arrests in late-2019 after the firing of Daniel Pantaleo, the former police officer who killed Eric Garner in 2014, and another slowdown in late-2014 after the killing of two police officers in Bed-Stuy.

“What they’re demonstrating by engaging in these types of slowdowns is that they’re making far too many arrests for things that don’t rise to the level of needing law enforcement intervention and don’t actually serve the public or help foster a safe and secure sense of community," says Vincent Southerland, the executive director of the Center on Race, Inequality, and the Law at NYU.
